# Healthcare's Missing Infrastructure
## The Foundation Where Domain-Native AI Can Actually Happen

![Healthcare's Missing Infrastructure](/assets/images/healthcare-infrastructure-hero.png)

In a November 2025 Forbes article "Why Generic AI Fails In Healthcare—And What Comes Next," Rajan Kohli, CEO of CitiusTech, delivered a stark diagnosis of healthcare AI's adoption crisis. Despite an explosion in AI experimentation over recent years, very few projects have made it into clinical routine or operational reality. The culprit? What Kohli identifies as an "illusion of readiness"—AI platforms built without healthcare's context embedded as core infrastructure. Generic systems treat clinical workflows, governance requirements, and stakeholder trust as afterthoughts, leading to pilots that never reach practice.

Kohli's prescription is clear: healthcare needs "domain-native" platforms where sector expertise isn't bolted on but built in from the foundation. But here's the question that follows: Where does this infrastructure actually live?

## The Infrastructure Problem Nobody's Talking About

Healthcare's challenges aren't just technical—they're organizational. Kohli outlines four critical realities that domain-agnostic platforms ignore:

**Data complexity:** Healthcare data lives in unstructured notes, image formats, clinical devices, and legacy systems. Extracting meaning requires more than parsing—it demands clinical literacy.

**Workflow intricacy:** From intake to diagnosis, billing to discharge, healthcare runs on a maze of handoffs, approvals, and human-in-the-loop moments that generic AI simply can't navigate.

**Governance demands:** Models must show how they got their results. Traceability, explainability, and policy alignment are regulatory and ethical requirements, not optional features.

**Life-or-death stakes:** As Kohli notes, "A missed insight in e-commerce might lose a sale. In healthcare, it might cost a life."

This raises a foundational question: where do these domain-native platforms actually exist? Healthcare's fragmented stakeholders need organized infrastructure where they can finally converge.

Consider the stakeholders in any specialty:

**Diabetes care** involves endocrinologists, patients, device manufacturers, dietitians, researchers, insurers, caregivers, and data scientists who need to connect, communicate, share knowledge, collaborate on research, and transact commerce.

**Cancer treatment** spans oncologists, radiologists, pathologists, pharma companies, patient advocates, clinical trial coordinators, genetic counselors, and support networks requiring organized space to converge.

**Mental health** touches psychiatrists, therapists, social workers, schools, employers, family support systems, and digital health innovators needing infrastructure that brings them together.

Today, these conversations happen everywhere and nowhere—closed proprietary EHR systems, academic silos, fragmented social media groups, disconnected commercial platforms, and isolated forums with no standardization or continuity.

There is no organized public infrastructure where healthcare's scattered stakeholders naturally converge to connect, communicate, and collaborate.

That's the foundation Kohli's domain-native AI actually needs. And that foundation doesn't exist yet.
